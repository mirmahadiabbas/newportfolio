The problem

The technology is rarely what fails

Organisations are spending trillions on digital transformation, yet around 70% of these initiatives fail to deliver what they promised. The research showed the failures rarely come from the technology itself. They come from rigid methodologies applied to fluid problems, fragmented tool ecosystems that bury visibility, and leadership that struggles to adapt to organisational and cultural realities. There is a persistent gap between what the theory recommends and what project managers can actually do inside real organisations.

How I researched it

Three months inside the problem

I spent three months embedded as a Project Management Intern in TUI Airlines' Business Development and Transformation team, working across live transformation programmes including the Advanced Crew Ecosystem. Alongside the day job, I conducted eleven semi-structured interviews with project and programme managers delivering change across TUI's European entities, then analysed them using a Gioia-informed thematic method. The study received formal ethics approval through Cranfield's research ethics system, and all participants were anonymised.

What I found

Four findings from the front line

Finding 01

Methodology is a negotiation, not a template

Nobody ran pure Agile or pure Waterfall. Managers blended both depending on stakeholders, regulation, and team maturity, often delivering iteratively while reporting through traditional milestones.

"You can't go full Agile or full Waterfall: report up one way and deliver down another."

Programme Manager, TUI Airlines
Finding 02

Tools enable and constrain in equal measure

Despite sophisticated platforms being available, Excel and PowerPoint remained the backbone. Fragmentation across tools created duplication and killed visibility.

"Excel and PowerPoint are our backbone."

Project Manager, TUI Airlines
Finding 03

Leadership is the integrator

The strongest finding: trust, empathy, and cultural fluency mattered more than any framework. Adaptive leaders held hybrid delivery together where process alone could not.

"Programme management is 70% leadership."

Group Portfolio & Programme Manager, TUI Airlines
Finding 04

Success is value, not just the iron triangle

Practitioners defined excellence as benefit realisation, adoption, and sustainable change, not just hitting time, cost, and scope.

"Tracking benefits matters more than hitting a timeline."

Group Portfolio & Programme Manager, TUI Airlines

The framework

A Strategic Framework for Delivery Excellence

The findings converge into a Strategic Framework for Delivery Excellence. It positions transformation success as the interaction of three pillars: methodological adaptation, technological integration, and leadership adaptability, resting on governance, team culture, and continuous learning. Delivery excellence emerges from the interplay, not from optimising any single pillar.

Delivery Excellence

  • Value creation and benefit realisation
  • Stakeholder trust and alignment
  • Sustainable organisational learning
Methodological Adaptation

Recommendations

Three strategic recommendations

01

Build a unified digital delivery ecosystem

An Integrated Delivery Management System built on the Microsoft Power Platform, connecting Teams, SharePoint, and Power BI into a single source of truth for planning, risk, and reporting.

02

Embed adaptive leadership as a core capability

A structured development framework using experiential learning, mentoring, and 360-degree feedback, so leadership agility grows alongside digital maturity.

03

Institutionalise value-centric measurement

A Delivery Excellence Framework combining traditional KPIs with benefit realisation, adoption rates, and stakeholder trust, embedded in project charters and governance.
